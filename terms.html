13.6 Export Risk — Credential Exposure in Source Code

This section is critically important. Read it carefully before using Connectors and exporting your site.

What "Use At Your Own Risk" Means

"Use at your own risk" is a legal term meaning that Tamato accepts zero responsibility for any negative outcome that results from your decision to export a site containing connector credentials. If something goes wrong — your accounts get hacked, money gets stolen, your data gets exposed, your connected services get misused — that is entirely your problem, not Tamato's. Tamato will not compensate you, reimburse you, assist you, or accept any legal liability. You made the choice to export. You accepted the risk. The consequences are yours alone.

What Happens When You Export

When you export a website that uses active Connectors, the following occurs automatically and irrevocably:

  • Your API keys, OAuth tokens, embed codes, account identifiers, and any other credentials associated with your connected services are written in plain, human-readable text directly into your HTML source code files
  • These credentials are not encrypted, obfuscated, hidden, or protected in any way within the exported files
  • The exported HTML files are standard text files that any person, program, search engine, or automated system can open and read
  • Once the export is downloaded to your device, Tamato has no control over those files whatsoever

What Plain Text Credentials Mean

Plain text credentials in an HTML file means that anyone who can access your file can see your credentials instantly by:

  • Opening the file in any text editor
  • Right-clicking any webpage hosted with the file and selecting "View Source"
  • Using any automated scanning tool, search engine crawler, or bot that indexes your site
  • Accessing your file through a shared drive, email attachment, file hosting service, or any other distribution method

This is not a bug or a flaw in Tamato's system. This is an inherent and unavoidable consequence of the nature of client-side HTML files and how browser-based websites work. No website builder can embed live third-party service credentials in a standalone HTML file and keep those credentials secret from anyone who can view the file.

Specific Risks of Credential Exposure

By exporting a site with active credentials you are accepting all of the following risks, without limitation:

(a) Unauthorized Account Access

Any person who obtains your exported HTML file can extract your API keys and OAuth tokens and use them to access your connected accounts — your Google account, your Stripe account, your Mailchimp lists, your PayPal account, your Notion workspace, your Instagram account, your Spotify account, your OpenTable account, and your Calendly account — without your knowledge or permission.

(b) Financial Loss

(c) Data Theft

Exposed Google Sheets, Mailchimp, and Notion credentials can be used to access, copy, modify, or delete your data, your customers' data, your mailing lists, and any other information stored in those services.

(d) Account Hijacking

Exposed OAuth tokens may allow third parties to take control of your connected accounts, change your credentials, lock you out, or impersonate you or your business.

(e) Spam and Abuse

Exposed Mailchimp credentials can be used to send spam emails from your account. Exposed Google credentials can be used to abuse your Google services. Exposed Spotify credentials can be used to manipulate your account. All resulting consequences — account suspension, legal liability, reputational damage — are yours alone.

(f) Cascading Security Breaches

A single exposed credential can lead to unauthorized access across multiple services if those services share authentication or if attackers use the initial access to gain further access to your systems.

If you host your exported site publicly, search engine crawlers may index your source code and make your credentials discoverable through public search engines. Tamato is not liable for any consequences of search engine indexing of your exported files.
